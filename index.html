Section 1

Section 1: Learning Objectives

After completing Topics 1 and 2, you should be able to:

  • Define epidemiology and explain the components of its definition.
  • Distinguish between descriptive and analytical epidemiology, and between observational and interventional approaches.
  • Articulate the three core functions of epidemiology.
  • Define what "population" means in epidemiological (rather than demographic) terms.
  • Describe any health-related event using the three dimensions of person, place, and time.
  • Distinguish between an exposure and an outcome in a given study.
  • Explain why case definitions matter and how they affect numerator counts.

Section 2

Section 2: What is Epidemiology?

2.1 The formal definition

Epidemiology is frequently in the news, often without being named as such. When you read a headline such as "HIV prevalence declines among young women in Zimbabwe" or "cholera cases concentrated in high-density Harare suburbs", you are reading the output of epidemiological thinking.

Definition

"The study of the occurrence and distribution of health-related states or events in specified populations, including the study of the determinants influencing such states, and the application of this study to the control of health problems."

Porta, Greenland & Last — Dictionary of Epidemiology, 5th ed., Oxford University Press, 2008.

Let us unpack the definition phrase by phrase, because each component carries important meaning.

Occurrence and distribution. We are interested not just in whether a disease exists, but in how common it is and how it is spread across different groups and places. For example, knowing that hypertension exists among people living with HIV (PLHIV) is not particularly informative — but knowing that its prevalence among PLHIV aged ≥50 at a Harare clinic is 41.2% versus an estimated 30% in the general Zimbabwean adult population gives us something to act on.

Health-related states or events. Our subject matter is deliberately broad. It encompasses diseases, injuries, health behaviours, biological measures (blood pressure, viral load, CD4 count), and even positive outcomes such as viral suppression or healthy birth weight. This breadth is important for clinicians: epidemiological reasoning applies whenever you are thinking about patterns rather than individual patients.

Specified populations. This is the feature that most distinguishes epidemiology from clinical medicine. A clinician cares for the individual patient in front of them; an epidemiologist asks how that patient's experience relates to the experience of a defined group. The population must be specified — it is the denominator that gives the numerator (cases) meaning.

Determinants. We seek to understand what drives the patterns we observe — biological, behavioural, social, economic, or environmental factors that increase or decrease risk.

Application and control. Epidemiology is not an academic exercise in pattern-description. Its purpose is to generate knowledge that can be translated into action: public health interventions, clinical guidelines, health policy, or programme evaluation.

Zimbabwean example — all parts of the definition

The IeDEA–Newlands cohort study exemplifies the full definition. Researchers at Newlands Clinic, Harare, studied the occurrence of new hypertension (health-related event) among HIV-positive adults on ART (specified population). They examined whether the dolutegravir (DTG) ART regimen was a determinant of hypertension through its effect on weight gain. Findings fed back into application: clinical monitoring protocols for PLHIV on DTG across Southern Africa. Every component of the definition is present.

2.2 The three core questions

A helpful way to remember what epidemiology does is through three fundamental questions that it asks of any health event:

QuestionWhat it drivesExample
How often? Measurement of frequency — prevalence, incidence, mortality What proportion of PLHIV aged ≥40 have hypertension?
In whom, where, when? Distribution — by person, place, and time Is hypertension more common in women or men, in Harare or rural districts, in 2010 vs 2020?
Why? Determinants — risk factors, exposures Is long ART duration, DTG use, or BMI the strongest predictor?

A fourth implicit question is "So what?" — the application question that distinguishes a purely academic exercise from public health practice.

2.3 Historical roots — why history matters for method

Understanding the history of epidemiology is not mere trivia. The founders of the discipline made methodological choices — how to define a case, what denominator to use, how to compare groups — that remain directly relevant to your work today.

Hippocrates (~460 BC). Writing in On Airs, Waters, and Places, Hippocrates described how disease patterns varied with season, climate, water source, and personal behaviour. His framework — that disease has environmental and behavioural determinants that vary by place and time — is recognisably modern. The word epidemiology derives from the Greek epi (upon) and demos (people).

William Farr (England, 1839). Farr established England's first systematic death-registration system, enabling comparison of mortality rates across occupational groups, geographic areas, and time periods. His approach is the template for every routine surveillance system that exists today — including Zimbabwe's District Health Information Software (DHIS2), which was used to track the 2023–24 cholera outbreak in real time.

Ignaz Semmelweis (Vienna, 1840s). Semmelweis observed high maternal mortality from puerperal fever in a ward staffed by medical students compared with one staffed by midwives. By noticing that medical students performed post-mortem examinations without handwashing before attending deliveries, he hypothesised that unwashed hands were transmitting infection. When he introduced chlorinated handwashing, mortality fell dramatically. This is one of the earliest recorded examples of a natural experiment in epidemiology.

John Snow (London, 1854). Snow's investigation of a cholera outbreak in Soho is perhaps the most celebrated case study in epidemiological history — and directly relevant to Zimbabwe, which has dealt with severe cholera outbreaks for decades. Working before germ theory was established, Snow mapped cholera deaths and compared death rates among households served by two different water companies. He found that households served by the Southwark and Vauxhall company experienced 31.5 deaths per 1,000 houses compared with 3.8 per 1,000 for the Lambeth company — roughly an 8-fold difference. This ratio is recognisable today as a form of rate ratio, a concept formalised in Module 1 Topic 3.

Methodological lesson from Snow

Snow used both descriptive epidemiology (mapping cases by location and time) and analytical epidemiology (comparing rates across water-supply groups). He did not prove that contaminated water causes cholera — epidemiologists provide evidence for or against a hypothesis, they do not deliver proof. But his evidence was strong enough to guide action. This distinction between association and causation is central to the discipline and will recur throughout this course.

2.4 Epidemiology in Zimbabwe today

Epidemiology is not a historical discipline. It is practised daily in Zimbabwe's health system, and many of you will encounter its outputs in your clinical work without necessarily identifying them as such.

The 2023–2024 cholera outbreak — epidemiology in action

On 12 February 2023, Zimbabwe's first cholera case of the year was reported in Chegutu, Mashonaland West. By August 2024, when the outbreak was officially declared over, a cumulative total of 34,549 suspected cases and 718 deaths had been recorded across all 10 provinces, with a case fatality rate of 2.1% (WHO, 2024).

The public health response required epidemiological thinking at every stage:

  • Surveillance and case counting — distinguishing suspected (clinical) from confirmed (laboratory) cases using a precise case definition, tracked through DHIS2.
  • Distribution analysis — identifying that Harare, Manicaland, and Mashonaland Central accounted for over 77% of cases, enabling targeted resource allocation.
  • Risk factor investigation — linking transmission to contaminated water supply, breakdown of sanitation infrastructure, and population displacement.
  • Intervention evaluation — over 2.4 million oral cholera vaccine doses were deployed; surveillance data monitored whether case incidence declined in vaccinated areas.

Sources: WHO AFRO, 2024; Marumure et al., Journal of Tropical Medicine, 2024.

Section 3

Section 3: Descriptive and Analytical Epidemiology

3.1 Descriptive epidemiology

Descriptive epidemiology

Studies designed to describe the frequency and distribution of an exposure or outcome, without seeking to explain the distribution by looking for associations.

Key question: "What is happening, and in whom, where, and when?"

Descriptive epidemiology is the essential first step in any investigation. Before we can ask why something is happening, we need to characterise what is happening. This requires:

  • A reliable numerator — an accurate count of cases, using a consistent case definition.
  • A reliable denominator — an accurate count of the population at risk, so that we can calculate a rate or proportion rather than just a raw count.
  • Stratification by person, place, and time (discussed in Section 5).
Descriptive example — Newlands Clinic elderly cohort

Chimbetete et al. (2020, Southern African Journal of HIV Medicine) conducted a cross-sectional analysis of 1,688 patients aged ≥50 years receiving HIV care at Newlands Clinic as of 1 October 2019 — 25.8% of the total clinic population. They reported: median ART duration 10.9 years; viral load <50 copies/mL in over 90%; hypertension prevalence 41.2%; arthritis 19.9%; chronic kidney disease 11.6%.

This is descriptive epidemiology: the study describes the frequency and distribution of conditions in a defined group. It does not test whether any factor caused the hypertension — that would require analytical methods.

Routine data sources are the backbone of descriptive epidemiology. In Zimbabwe, these include:

  • DHIS2 — the national Health Management Information System used for routine facility-level data.
  • ZIMSTAT — the Zimbabwe National Statistics Agency, including census data.
  • The Zimbabwe Population-Based HIV Impact Assessment (ZIMPHIA) — repeated nationally representative HIV surveys.
  • Clinic-based cohort databases such as the IeDEA–Newlands dataset.

3.2 Analytical epidemiology

Analytical epidemiology

Studies designed to examine associations between exposures and outcomes, often with the aim of identifying possible causes of an outcome.

Key question: "Why is this pattern occurring? What is driving it?"

Also called aetiological studies.

Analytical epidemiology moves beyond description to comparison. We compare groups — those exposed to a risk factor versus those not exposed, or those who developed the outcome versus those who did not — to quantify the association between the exposure and the outcome.

Analytical example — DTG and hypertension at Newlands Clinic

Shamu et al. (2022, JIAS) studied 9,487 PLHIV at Newlands Clinic starting or switching to dolutegravir (DTG), efavirenz (EFV), or atazanavir/ritonavir (ATV/r). Over two years, DTG was associated with weight gain of approximately 4 kg, compared with lower gains on the other regimens. The proportion with hypertension continued to increase in the DTG group. Statistical models adjusted for age, sex, baseline BMI, and CD4 count.

This is analytical epidemiology: a specific association is tested, confounding is addressed, and an estimate of association size is produced. It demonstrates association — it does not prove causation.

A central caution in analytical epidemiology: association does not equal causation. John Snow found that altitude above sea level was associated with cholera — but this was because lower-lying areas happened to be served by contaminated water. Farr's "miasma" theory was a plausible but incorrect causal interpretation of a genuine association. The discipline of causal inference will be covered in later modules.

3.3 Observational vs interventional epidemiology

TypeDefinitionSubtypesZimbabwe example
Observational We examine the distribution or determinants of an outcome without attempting to influence them. Can be descriptive or analytical Cross-sectional survey of hypertension prevalence among PLHIV at Newlands Clinic
Interventional We modify an exposure within the study population and measure the effect on the outcome. Always analytical Randomised trial testing community-based BP screening vs standard care in Harare

Randomised controlled trials (RCTs) can provide stronger evidence for causal relationships than observational studies, because randomisation balances known and unknown confounders. However, RCTs are expensive, ethically constrained, and sometimes infeasible. Most of the knowledge base in clinical epidemiology in sub-Saharan Africa is built from observational studies.

Think and reflect

James Lind's 1747 scurvy experiment at sea is considered one of the first controlled clinical trials in history. He gave 12 scurvy patients different dietary supplements and measured outcomes over six days. Those given citrus fruits recovered; others did not. Would you classify this as observational or interventional? Descriptive or analytical? Discuss with a colleague before reading on.

Answer: Interventional (Lind actively modified the exposure — the dietary supplement) and analytical (he compared outcomes across groups to identify a causal factor).

Section 4

Section 4: The Role of Epidemiology

4.1 Three core functions

The purpose of epidemiology is ultimately practical. Its three core functions can be understood as a cycle:

Function 1: Describe. Quantify the frequency and distribution of health events in a population. This gives us the baseline — who is affected, where, and when. Without accurate description, we cannot identify priorities, allocate resources, or detect emerging threats. The ZIMPHIA surveys, which periodically estimate HIV prevalence and incidence across Zimbabwe, are a national-scale example of descriptive epidemiology serving a planning function.

Function 2: Interpret. Investigate risk factors and determinants by examining associations between exposures and outcomes. This is where analytical methods — regression models, survival analyses, measures of association — come in. Understanding that PLHIV on longer-duration ART carry a higher burden of hypertension and type 2 diabetes informs integrated NCD screening protocols.

Function 3: Apply and evaluate. Translate findings into health interventions, guidelines, or policies. Then evaluate whether they work — which is itself an epidemiological exercise. The evaluation of Zimbabwe's oral cholera vaccine campaign during the 2023–24 outbreak is a current example.

All three functions — hypertension cascade at Newlands Clinic

Describe: The Newlands cohort documented that hypertension prevalence among PLHIV aged ≥50 is 41.2%, substantially higher than in younger patients.

Interpret: Analytical work investigated whether this excess burden was explained by ART duration, regimen choice (particularly DTG), increasing BMI over time, or background age-related cardiovascular risk.

Apply and evaluate: These findings contributed to integrated HIV-NCD care pathways at Newlands Clinic, and provided the evidence base for the EVAH grant proposal (AI-enabled cardiovascular screening for PLHIV in Zimbabwe and Zambia).

4.2 Limitations — confounding and error

Epidemiology is not conducted in a controlled laboratory. We work with real populations in complex social environments where multiple factors operate simultaneously and are often correlated with each other. This creates two fundamental challenges:

Confounding. A confounder is a third variable that is associated with both the exposure and the outcome, and can create a spurious association or mask a real one. Farr's elevation-altitude-cholera example is the textbook case. In the IeDEA–Newlands DTG study, age and baseline BMI are confounders — statistical adjustment was required to estimate the independent association of DTG with hypertension.

Bias and error. Systematic errors can arise in how we select study participants (selection bias), how we measure exposures and outcomes (information bias), or how we lose participants to follow-up (attrition bias). Recognising and minimising these sources of error is a core skill developed over subsequent modules.

Section 5

Section 5: The Epidemiological Focus — Populations

5.1 Defining the population

The key question in epidemiology is always: "What is the denominator?"

In demography, "population" means the inhabitants of a geographic area. In epidemiology, the population is the defined collection of units from which our sample is drawn. These units are most commonly people, but they could be clinical consultations, households, or health facilities. The epidemiological population is defined by its characteristics, not just its geography.

Consider the difference between these two statements:

  • "There were 50 new HIV diagnoses in Harare in June 2026." — A count (numerator only). Without knowing the denominator, we cannot interpret whether 50 is high or low.
  • "The HIV incidence rate among sexually active adults aged 15–49 in Harare in 2026 was estimated at 0.8 per 100 person-years." — A rate with a defined denominator: interpretable and comparable.
Always ask: what is the denominator?

A clinician presents a poster: "We saw 80 cases of peripheral arterial disease in our clinic this year." An epidemiologist immediately asks: out of how many patients? What was the population at risk? What was the follow-up time? Without the denominator, the 80 is descriptively interesting but epidemiologically uninformative.

5.2 Person, place, and time

The fundamental framework for describing the distribution of a health event is to characterise it across three dimensions: person, place, and time.

Person refers to the characteristics of individuals: age, sex, occupation, socioeconomic status, HIV status, ART regimen, BMI, smoking status, and so on. Person characteristics help identify which groups carry the greatest burden and may generate hypotheses about risk factors.

Place refers to geographic or institutional location: country, province, urban versus rural, catchment clinic, residential type. Geographic patterns often reflect differences in environmental exposures, healthcare access, or population characteristics.

Time refers to when events occur: the calendar period, secular trends, seasonal patterns, or epidemic curves. Time patterns can reveal the incubation period of an infectious disease, the lag between exposure and outcome in chronic disease, or the impact of an intervention.

Worked example — identify person, place, and time

Statement: "The prevalence of hypertension among HIV-positive women aged 50–59 receiving ART at Newlands Clinic, Harare, as of 1 October 2019, was higher than among men in the same age group."

  • Person: HIV-positive adults on ART aged 50–59, with comparison by sex.
  • Place: Newlands Clinic, Harare.
  • Time: Cross-sectional — 1 October 2019.

Knowing the place is important: Newlands Clinic serves a predominantly employed urban population with long ART duration. The results may not generalise to rural public sector clinics.

Worked example 2 — cholera outbreak 2023–2024

Statement: "As of 11 February 2024, cumulative cholera cases were highest in Harare (including Chitungwiza), Manicaland, and Masvingo, accounting for 77.4% of all cases. The outbreak began on 12 February 2023 in Chegutu and spread to all 10 provinces within 12 months."

  • Person: Not specified in this extract — surveillance data stratified by age and sex were collected separately.
  • Place: Zimbabwe, all 10 provinces; highest burden in Harare/Chitungwiza, Manicaland, Masvingo.
  • Time: February 2023 to February 2024; weekly epidemic curve showed rainy-season peaks.

Section 6

Section 6: Defining Cases, Exposures, and Outcomes

6.1 Case definitions

A case definition is the set of clinical, laboratory, and/or epidemiological criteria that must be met for a person to be counted as a "case" in a study or surveillance system. It determines who goes in the numerator.

Case definitions must balance two competing concerns:

  • Sensitivity: capturing all true cases (avoiding false negatives). A more sensitive definition casts a wider net — useful for outbreak detection.
  • Specificity: excluding non-cases (avoiding false positives). A stricter definition is useful for estimating true disease prevalence.
Why case definitions matter — a cautionary example

In Zimbabwe's cholera surveillance, distinguishing a suspected case (acute watery diarrhoea in an area with known cholera activity) from a confirmed case (laboratory isolation of Vibrio cholerae) produced very different numbers: 34,549 suspected versus 4,217 confirmed cases. The choice of case definition changes the numerator — and therefore every rate or proportion derived from it. Studies using different definitions cannot be directly compared without this caveat.

Case definition — hypertension in the Newlands cohort

In the Newlands Clinic cohort (IeDEA-SA), hypertension was defined as: a documented systolic BP >140 mmHg and/or diastolic BP >90 mmHg measured on at least two different days, or current use of antihypertensive medication.

Requiring two readings on two different days reduces misclassification from isolated elevated measurements. Including antihypertensive use ensures that treated hypertensives with controlled BP are not misclassified as "no hypertension".

6.2 Exposures and outcomes

Two variables are measured in virtually every epidemiological study:

Exposure (risk factor / determinant)

Any factor that may influence the outcome — biological, behavioural, environmental, structural, or pharmacological. The exposure precedes the outcome in time (or is hypothesised to).

Outcome

The disease, health event, biological state, or behaviour that we are interested in. Does not have to be a "bad" outcome: viral suppression and healthy birth weight are outcomes too.

A crucial insight: the same variable can be an exposure in one study and an outcome in another, depending on the research question.

Research questionExposureOutcome
Does smoking cause lung cancer? Cigarette smoking Lung cancer
Does cigarette advertising increase smoking uptake? Exposure to advertising Cigarette smoking
Does a midwife's advice increase breastfeeding? Midwife's advice Breastfeeding initiation
Does breastfeeding reduce diarrhoeal disease? Breastfeeding Diarrhoeal disease
Does DTG-based ART cause weight gain? (Newlands) ART regimen (DTG vs EFV) Weight gain / hypertension
Does weight gain mediate the DTG–hypertension link? Weight gain Hypertension

In Snow's cholera investigation, the exposure was the water source (Southwark and Vauxhall vs Lambeth Company). The outcome was death from cholera. Elevation above sea level — initially studied by Farr — turned out to be a proxy for water supply: a confounder, not a cause.

Think and reflect — identify the exposure and outcome

A research team at Newlands Clinic wants to investigate whether alcohol use (AUDIT-C score ≥3) is associated with failure to achieve viral suppression (HIV RNA >200 copies/mL at 12 months on ART).

  1. What is the exposure?
  2. What is the outcome?
  3. Name one potential confounder and explain why it might be one.

Suggested answers:

  1. Alcohol use (AUDIT-C ≥3).
  2. Viral non-suppression at 12 months on ART.
  3. Possible confounders: socioeconomic status (associated with both higher alcohol use and poorer adherence); depression (associated with both alcohol use and non-adherence); sex (men drink more and may have different adherence patterns).

Test your understanding — Topics 1 & 2

  1. Write your own one-sentence definition of epidemiology. Compare it to the Porta 2008 definition and identify any components you missed.
  2. A colleague says: "We had 120 patients with hypertension at our clinic this year." What critical piece of information is missing, and why does it matter?
  3. Classify each as descriptive or analytical, and observational or interventional:
    1. A survey of HIV prevalence among pregnant women attending antenatal care in Bulawayo in 2024.
    2. A cohort study comparing time to viral suppression among PLHIV receiving DTG-based versus EFV-based ART at Newlands Clinic.
    3. A randomised trial testing whether community health worker visits improve blood pressure control in hypertensive PLHIV in Harare.
  4. "The incidence of tuberculosis among male mineworkers aged 30–55 working in gold mines in Matabeleland South between 2018 and 2022 was 4.2 per 100 person-years." — Identify person, place, and time.
  5. In a study examining whether elevated BMI at ART initiation predicts new-onset hypertension over 5 years, what is the exposure and what is the outcome? Could hypertension be an exposure in a different study? Give an example.
  6. Why might the case fatality rate using suspected cases (n=34,549) differ from one using confirmed cases (n=4,217) in the 2023–24 Zimbabwe cholera outbreak? Which denominator is more appropriate for which purpose?

Outline answers (not comprehensive):

2. The denominator — the population at risk — is missing. Without it we cannot calculate a rate or proportion, and cannot tell whether 120 is high or low.

3a. Descriptive, observational.   3b. Analytical, observational.   3c. Analytical, interventional.

4. Person: male mineworkers aged 30–55.   Place: gold mines in Matabeleland South.   Time: 2018–2022.

5. Exposure: BMI at ART initiation. Outcome: new-onset hypertension. Yes — e.g. in a study of whether hypertension causes accelerated kidney disease among PLHIV, hypertension becomes the exposure and CKD the outcome.

6. Using suspected cases gives a lower CFR because the denominator is larger. Using confirmed cases gives a higher CFR but covers only laboratory-confirmed cases. The suspected-case CFR is better for population-level burden estimates; the confirmed-case CFR is better for clinical severity characterisation.

Section 8

Section 8: Summary

What is epidemiology?

The study of occurrence, distribution, and determinants of health events in specified populations, applied to disease control.

Descriptive vs analytical

Descriptive: what, who, where, when? Analytical: why? Both can be observational; only interventional studies are always analytical.

Three functions

Describe frequency and distribution → Interpret associations and determinants → Apply findings and evaluate interventions.

The epidemiological population

A defined group of units from which the sample is drawn — not a geographic concept. Always identify the denominator.

Person, place, and time

The three dimensions for describing any health event. Every epidemiological statement should specify all three.

Case definitions, exposures & outcomes

Case definitions determine the numerator. The same variable can be exposure in one study and outcome in another — context is everything.
